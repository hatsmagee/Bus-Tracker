🚌 About this dashboard
A live map of every Hele-On Bus in service right now, built on the County's official GTFS-realtime feed. It reads the whole fleet from one VehiclePositions feed, shows the agency's own predicted arrival times (TripUpdates), and layers a small transformer on top that learns each stop's typical timing from history.
🎨 How to read it
Bus icons glide smoothly along the road between GPS updates (snapped to the route, moving forward at the bus's real pace) with the route number always upright. Buses that overlap fan out so none is hidden, and ones whose GPS has gone quiet fade to a ghost at their last-known spot. Blue stops are official GTFS stops — click one for approaching buses and history. Amber rings are observed stops: spots where buses repeatedly stop that aren't official, learned from GPS history. Direction chevrons stream ahead of each bus along the road, showing where it's about to go. A weather symbol above each bus shows live conditions there, and the colored bar shows how full it is. The Fleet tab lists every bus the agency knows — live, idle, or dormant — with full telemetry. The Boats tab shows live vessel positions (requires a free aisstream.io key) and the Aircraft tab shows live ADS-B positions around the islands via OpenSky Network (no key needed). The Hawaiʻi tab layers in public federal/state data: recent USGS earthquakes, active NWS watches/warnings, and lava flow hazard zones / tsunami evacuation zones from the Hawaii Statewide GIS Program — all free and keyless.

🛣️ Route lines
Route lines are snapped onto a routable graph built directly from a local OpenStreetMap road extract for Hawai'i Island — every intersection becomes a graph node, every block a graph edge. Each GTFS route shape is snapped onto that graph and stitched together via shortest-path along real road segments, so a drawn route line is composed entirely of actual road geometry — it can't cut a diagonal across a block, because there's no code path that draws anything other than a real OSM way. Where local map data genuinely has a gap (a few remote rural stretches), the line breaks honestly rather than bridging with a straight line. Where two or more routes share a road, they draw as clean parallel lanes scaled to the number sharing it, offset only where the sharing actually happens (not for the route's whole length).
🧠 The predictor
Headline arrival times come straight from the agency's official realtime predictions. On top of those, a 12-token single-head transformer (trained server-side every time a bus reaches a stop) learns a correction from history. Inputs are cyclical time-of-day and day-of-week (sin/cos), distance to the next stop, and speed; outputs are ETA adjustments for the next 5 stops.
